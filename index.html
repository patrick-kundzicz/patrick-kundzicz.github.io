Spectrogram Exploration of Music Genre Samples From 2000-2001

Built using the GTZAN dataset: 1000 songs categorized by genre
The dataset contains audio (22050 Hz, 16-bit, mono), spectrograms, and a set of features for each song

Chroma STFT (Short-Time Fourier Transform) Mean
- Groups the frequency range (0-11025 Hz) into 12 bins and averages the resulting value over our audio sample
- Commonly used in tools for key or harmonic detection of a song

RMS (Root Mean-Square) Loudness
- Measures the average amplitude of an audio sample
- Gives an idea of (perceived) loudness and is a commonly-used metric when mixing and mastering of a song
to make sure the dynamic/transient content is in-line with other tracks in the genre

Guided exploration: How are RMS, Chroma STFT, and Spectrogram visualizations relevant to music production

Click on a datapoint with a high RMS Mean value and a low Chroma STFT Mean.
- In the spectrogram, there are generally more bright regions for longer time periods
- The spectrogram will also generally have more high frequencies if we compare to a low RMS Mean datapoint
- Filling out the entire frequency spectrum with more content can lend itself to creating a louder track (although not a strict rule)

Click on a datapoint with a low RMS Mean value and a high Chroma STFT Mean.
- Many songs are constructed using scales built on 12 degrees
- Each scale degree across the different octaves and its overtones can be converted to a corresponding frequency
- Because of this relationship, we can average out the frequency content of a song into a single value, and understand the general harmonic content
- The samples chosen for "disco" have a higher variance in chroma than rock, which suggests that the harmonic content of the disco tracks are more varied

Why is this important to understand for people involved in the engineering of music
- Generally, when music is made, we draw on references from our previous listening and try to create something new
- When working on a song that falls under the umbrella of "metal", a producer will want the loudness and dynamic/transient content of that track to stand up to other metal tracks
- The same producer working on a "classical" track would likely want that mix to stand up to other "classical" tracks, requiring a different set of references
- Being mindful of common characteristics (like RMS loudness) in the genres you pull from are important to creating songs that stand up when played next to others in the genre
